# Cognitive Debt

## Definition
The hypothesis that reliance on AI assistance produces short-term productivity gains at the cost of long-term cognitive skill degradation — analogous to financial debt where immediate benefit is borrowed against future capacity.

## Key Arguments
The [[cognitive-debt-ai-brain|MIT Media Lab study]] provides the first neuroscientific evidence: EEG data showed reduced brain connectivity in LLM users, with 83% unable to recall their own AI-assisted writing. The effect is cumulative and persists after AI removal.

However, critical nuances exist: (1) reduced brain activity may indicate efficiency, not impairment; (2) the sample was small (54 participants); (3) historical precedent shows similar fears with every new technology (calculators, typewriters, internet); (4) when brain-only writers later used AI, their brain regions reactivated — suggesting the effect is reversible with proper training.

Sung's key insight: the real risk is not that "AI rots brains" but that we need to **use AI better in teaching and learning**. The pedagogical response should focus on process-based assessment, AI literacy, and metacognitive skill development.
