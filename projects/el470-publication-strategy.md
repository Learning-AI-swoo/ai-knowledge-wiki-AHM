# EL470 Publication Strategy

## Overview
Strategy for publishing in medical education journals (Medical Education, BMC Medical Education, Academic Medicine, etc.) with AI-focused **clarification research** — the shift from "does it work?" to "why does it happen?"

The publication model comes directly from [[ai-published-medical-education|Dr. Kıyak's journey]] (#046): Medical Education editor Kevin Eva explicitly favors "clarification" studies over effectiveness demonstrations. This means grounding work in strong theoretical frameworks (e.g., Judge-Advisor System) and designing for clean experimental methodology (RCT preferred).

## Publication Angles

Ranked by strength of wiki evidence and alignment with clarification research approach:

### Angle 1: Metacognitive Laziness in Medical AI Education
**Sources**: [[metacognitive-laziness-ai]] (#062), [[cognitive-debt-ai-brain]] (#008)
**Framework**: Fan et al.'s "metacognitive laziness" + Sung's System 1/2 confusion analysis
**Core finding**: AI improves essay/task quality but NOT learning — the product gets better while the student doesn't
**Sung's unique contribution**: the false sense of cognitive confidence when AI chatbot speed collapses slow metacognitive assessment (System 2) into fast heuristic response (System 1)
**Design**: RCT comparing AI-assisted vs. unassisted clinical reasoning tasks with process mining of metacognitive behaviors

### Angle 2: AI Credibility Threshold in Medical Students
**Sources**: [[ai-published-medical-education]] (#046)
**Framework**: Judge-Advisor System (JAS) theory
**Core finding**: ChatGPT warning had zero effect because students already distrust AI below the "credibility threshold" (weight of advice: 0.15 vs. typical 0.30)
**Extension opportunity**: replicate with TCM students or different clinical contexts; test whether credibility threshold varies by AI system, task type, or cultural context

### Angle 3: AI as Problem-Solver AND Problem-Creator in Higher Ed
**Sources**: [[ai-impact-higher-education]] (#048), [[ai-future-education-unesco]] (#040)
**Framework**: UNESCO's dual-role analysis
**Core finding**: AI simultaneously solves problems (access, personalization) and creates new ones (skill atrophy, integrity challenges, equity gaps)
**Design**: qualitative study mapping how medical faculty experience this dual role in their own teaching

### Angle 4: Assessment Revolution — Beyond "End of Exams"
**Sources**: [[ai-future-education-unesco]] (#040), [[ai-rethinking-academic-integrity]] (#052), [[genai-transparency-framework-education]] (#075)
**Framework**: UNESCO's formative-summative integration model
**Core finding**: AI makes traditional assessment obsolete; the shift to process-based evidence portfolios is inevitable but under-implemented
**Design**: action research implementing portfolio-based assessment in a medical education course

### Angle 5: AI Time-Saving Illusion
**Sources**: [[time-saving-illusion-ai]] (#064), [[ai-assistance-skill-formation]] (#077), [[does-ai-save-time]] (#078)
**Framework**: Shen & Tamkin's skill formation model
**Core finding**: AI doesn't actually save time; interaction time offsets gains; delegation-heavy use reduces learning while explanation-seeking use preserves it
**Design**: time-motion study of medical students using AI for clinical reasoning tasks
